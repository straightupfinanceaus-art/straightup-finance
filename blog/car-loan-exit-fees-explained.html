Car Loan Exit Fees Explained: What to Watch Out For

Published February 4, 2026 · 5 min read

So you've come into some money and want to pay off your car loan early. Great idea — but before you do, it's worth checking if there are any exit fees that could eat into your savings.

What Are Exit Fees?

Exit fees (also called early termination fees or payout fees) are charges that some lenders apply when you pay off your loan before the end of its term. They're designed to compensate the lender for the interest they'll miss out on.

How Much Are Exit Fees?

Exit fees vary between lenders, but here's what's typical:

  • Fixed fee: Many lenders charge a flat fee of $200 - $800 to exit early
  • Sliding scale: Some fees reduce over time (e.g., $600 in year 1, $400 in year 2, etc.)
  • No exit fees: Some lenders don't charge any early payout fees

The Trade-Off

Lenders with no exit fees sometimes have slightly higher interest rates. It's a trade-off — you might pay a bit more in interest for the flexibility to exit without penalty.

Other Charges to Watch For

Exit fees aren't the only thing to consider when paying off early:

Break Costs

For fixed-rate loans, some lenders charge "break costs" if interest rates have dropped since you took out the loan. These can be significant.

Administration Fees

Some lenders charge a small fee ($20-$50) to process the payout and discharge the security over your vehicle.

Payout Figure vs Balance

Your payout figure might be slightly different from your remaining balance. Always request an official payout figure from your lender.

How to Avoid Exit Fee Pain

  1. Check before you sign — Read the loan contract and understand the exit fee structure before committing.
  2. Ask your broker — A good broker will explain all the fees, not just the interest rate.
  3. Do the maths — If you think you might pay off early, calculate whether a loan with no exit fees (but slightly higher rate) works out better.
  4. Refinance carefully — If you're refinancing to a better rate, make sure the savings outweigh any exit fees on your current loan.

The Bottom Line

Exit fees are a real cost that can catch people off guard. Most lenders charge something (typically up to $800), but the fee often decreases over the life of the loan. A few lenders offer no exit fees, usually with slightly higher rates.
