Broker vs Bank vs Dealer Finance: Which is Best?

Published February 4, 2026 · 6 min read

When it comes to financing a car, you've got three main options: use a finance broker, go to your bank, or take finance through the car dealer. Each has its pros and cons.

Let's break them down honestly.

Option 1: Finance Broker

How It Works

A broker acts as a middleman between you and multiple lenders. They compare options from their panel of lenders and present you with the best deals.

Pros

  • Choice — Access to multiple lenders means more options and better chance of a good rate
  • Expertise — Brokers know which lenders suit different circumstances
  • Less legwork — One application can be assessed by multiple lenders
  • Independence — A good broker works for you, not the lender or dealer

Cons

  • Quality varies — Some brokers mark up rates or push products that pay them more commission
  • Not face-to-face — Most broker interactions are over phone/online (though many prefer this)

Our Approach

At Straight Up Finance, we don't mark up rates — we pass on the lender's base rate to you. We charge the same origination fee regardless of your circumstances, so we have no incentive to push you into a more expensive product.

Option 2: Your Bank

How It Works

You apply for a car loan directly through your existing bank. They assess your application and offer a loan (or decline it).

Pros

  • Existing relationship — They know your history, which can help
  • Convenience — Everything in one place if you already bank with them
  • Trust — Big banks are familiar and regulated

Cons

  • Limited options — You only see what one lender offers
  • Not always competitive — Banks aren't always the cheapest, especially for car loans
  • Slow — Banks can take longer to process and approve
  • Rigid criteria — If you don't fit their box, you're out

Option 3: Dealer Finance

How It Works

The car dealership arranges finance for you, usually through their in-house finance manager who works with a panel of lenders.

Pros

  • Convenient — One-stop shop for car and finance
  • Fast — They want to close the deal, so they move quickly
  • Special offers — Sometimes manufacturers offer promotional rates through dealers

Cons

  • Conflict of interest — The dealer makes money from finance, so they may not offer you the best deal
  • Pressure — You're often pressured to decide on the spot
  • Mark-ups — Dealers commonly add margin to the interest rate
  • Add-ons — They often push expensive add-on products (warranty, insurance) that may not be good value

So Which Is Best?

There's no one-size-fits-all answer, but here's a general guide:

  • Use a broker if you want choice, competitive rates, and someone working in your interest
  • Use your bank if you have a strong existing relationship and they're offering a competitive deal
  • Be cautious with dealer finance — it can be convenient, but always compare to other options first

The best approach? Get a pre-approval from a broker before you go car shopping. That way, you know what you qualify for and can compare any dealer offer against it.

The Bottom Line

A good broker should save you money and hassle. But not all brokers are equal — look for one that doesn't mark up rates and is transparent about fees.
